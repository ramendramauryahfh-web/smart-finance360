AWS Outage vs Afghanistan Internet Blackout – Lessons on Digital Fragility

In October 2025, the world witnessed two very different but equally alarming digital disruptions: a major outage at Amazon Web Services (AWS) and an internet blackout in Afghanistan. Though their causes differ—one being technical and the other political—both demonstrate how interconnected and fragile our digital infrastructure has become.

1. The AWS Outage

Amazon Web Services, the world’s largest cloud provider, experienced a large-scale outage affecting multiple regions, primarily US-EAST-1. This led to widespread downtime across several popular platforms, apps, and services that rely on AWS.

Reports from various sources indicated:

  • Users globally faced login errors, app crashes, and failed transactions.
  • The root cause was linked to DNS resolution issues impacting the DynamoDB API endpoint.
  • Major companies using AWS saw cascading failures due to over-dependence on a single region.
  • AWS engineers implemented mitigations and reported gradual recovery after several hours.

This event showed how much of the global digital economy rests on a few centralized infrastructures.

2. The Afghanistan Internet Blackout

Around the same time, Afghanistan faced a completely different kind of digital disruption—a near-nationwide internet shutdown. The blackout, lasting more than nine days, was attributed to both fiber cuts and government-imposed restrictions. The move was reportedly linked to efforts to control online content and communication.

Key facts include:

  • Nearly the entire population lost access to the internet and mobile networks.
  • Humanitarian and media operations were severely affected.
  • The shutdown caused major communication gaps between provinces and with the outside world.
  • Restoration was gradual, and transparency about the cause was limited.

While AWS’s outage was an unintentional technical failure, Afghanistan’s blackout was a deliberate or politically driven action, showing how digital connectivity can be switched off as a form of control.

Both disruptions underline a key reality: whether caused by technical errors or human decisions, digital systems are fragile and centralized points of failure.

4. Broader Implications

Systemic Dependency:

Businesses rely on cloud ecosystems like AWS, which centralize massive portions of global traffic. A single failure can affect millions instantly.

Digital Freedom & Policy Risks:

Governments can—and sometimes do—exercise control over internet access. This undermines digital rights and can isolate entire nations.

Resilience Planning:

Organizations must build redundancy, spread workloads across multiple providers, and avoid over-reliance on a single region or vendor.

Human Cost:

For citizens, an internet blackout is not just a technical issue—it halts education, healthcare, business, and access to information.

5. Lessons Learned

  • Diversify cloud infrastructure. Relying on a single provider or region is risky.
  • Establish backup communication channels. Especially critical for media and NGOs.
  • Governments must protect connectivity. The internet is now a basic utility, not a privilege.
  • Transparency and accountability are vital in both corporate and government-level digital disruptions.

6. Final Thoughts

The AWS outage and Afghanistan’s blackout are two sides of the same coin—both highlight our dependence on complex digital systems. One shows the risks of technical centralization, and the other, the power of political control over connectivity.

As the world becomes more digital, resilience, decentralization, and digital rights must move from being “nice to have” ideals to urgent priorities.
